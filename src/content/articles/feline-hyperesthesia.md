Some cats have sudden, dramatic episodes: the **skin along their back ripples**, their pupils go wide, they whip around to bite at their own tail or flank, then tear off across the room. It's often mistaken for the "zoomies," but it may be **feline hyperesthesia syndrome** — nicknamed **twitchy cat syndrome**.

## What hyperesthesia looks like

During an episode, owners typically see some combination of:

- **Rolling or rippling skin** over the back and tail base
- **Frantic grooming, licking or biting** at the flank, back or tail
- **Dilated pupils** and a wide-eyed, agitated look
- **Sudden running, jumping or vocalising**
- A cat that seems **unable to settle**, then abruptly returns to normal

Episodes are usually brief but can be intense, and some cats injure themselves biting at their tail.

## Zoomies vs. hyperesthesia

This is the distinction that matters most:

| Normal zoomies | Hyperesthesia episode |
| --- | --- |
| Relaxed, playful body language | Agitated, distressed, wide eyes |
| Triggered by play or energy | Often triggered by touch along the back |
| No self-directed biting | Bites or attacks own tail/flank |
| Cat is happy afterwards | Cat may seem disturbed or sensitive |

If your cat looks **distressed** rather than playful, and targets its own body, that points toward hyperesthesia.

## Why it happens

The cause isn't fully understood, and hyperesthesia is often a **diagnosis of exclusion**. Vets look for the things that can trigger or mimic it:

- **Skin problems and itch** — fleas and allergies causing intense sensitivity
- **Pain** — especially in the spine, tail base or back
- **Neurological factors** — abnormal nerve signalling
- **Stress and compulsive behaviour** — anxiety-driven overgrooming

In many cats, more than one of these overlaps, which is why a thorough work-up matters.

## How vets approach it

Because "twitchy cat syndrome" can be a **symptom of something treatable**, the vet's job is to investigate rather than label:

1. A full physical and skin exam, plus strict flea control
2. Assessment for pain, especially along the spine and tail
3. Neurological evaluation and tests where indicated
4. A look at the cat's environment and stress levels

Treatment then targets the underlying issue: **flea and allergy control**, **pain relief**, **environmental enrichment** to reduce stress, and in some cases **medication** to calm nerve signalling or compulsive behaviour.

> The takeaway: don't assume dramatic back-twitching and tail-attacking is just a quirky personality. Recurring episodes are worth a vet visit, because many causes are very treatable.
