Every cat scratches sometimes. But a cat that scratches, chews or licks **all the time** is telling you something is wrong — and the cause that slips past many owners is an **allergy** called feline atopic dermatitis.

## Cats hide their itch

Dogs scratch where you can see them. Cats often **groom in private**, so instead of watching your cat scratch, you notice the aftermath:

- **Bald patches**, especially on the belly and inner legs
- **Scabs and tiny crusty bumps** along the back and neck
- **Overgrooming** that leaves the coat thin, broken or "barbered"
- A cat that seems **restless, irritable, or suddenly grooms after eating or coming inside**

Because these signs build up slowly, itching is easy to write off as stress or a quirk — when it's really a treatable medical problem.

## The three big causes of an itchy cat

Almost all persistent feline itch comes down to three overlapping causes:

1. **Fleas** — the single most common and most under-estimated trigger. In an allergic cat, one bite can cause days of itching, even if you never see a flea.
2. **Food allergy** — usually year-round itching, sometimes with digestive signs. Diagnosed with a strict diet trial.
3. **Environmental allergy (atopic dermatitis)** — reaction to pollen, dust mites and mould. Often seasonal at first, then constant.

The "allergy many owners miss" is usually **atopic dermatitis**, because it's a diagnosis of exclusion — you only reach it after ruling out fleas and food.

## Why you shouldn't just treat it at home

It's tempting to reach for a cream or a home remedy, but:

- Many **human anti-itch products and steroids are toxic to cats**
- Untreated itching leads to **secondary skin infections** that make everything worse
- The wrong treatment can **mask** the real cause and delay proper control

## What a vet will actually do

A vet approaches the itchy cat like a detective:

- Start **strict, year-round flea prevention** — this alone resolves a surprising number of cases
- **Examine and treat** any secondary bacterial or yeast infection
- Run a **food elimination trial** if itching is year-round
- Once other causes are excluded, offer **allergy testing** and a long-term plan, which may include prescription anti-itch medication or **immunotherapy**

> The key message: constant itching is not normal, it's rarely "just behaviour," and it's very treatable once the real trigger is found.
