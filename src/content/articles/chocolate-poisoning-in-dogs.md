Chocolate is one of the most common causes of dog poisoning — especially around holidays. If your dog has just eaten chocolate, the most important message is simple: **don't wait for symptoms. Call a vet now.**

## Why chocolate is toxic to dogs

Chocolate contains **theobromine** (and caffeine), stimulants that dogs metabolise very slowly. It builds up and overstimulates the heart and nervous system. Humans clear theobromine quickly; dogs don't — which is why something harmless to us can poison them.

## Not all chocolate is equal

The **darker and more bitter** the chocolate, the more theobromine — and the more dangerous:

| Type | Theobromine level | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cocoa powder / baking chocolate | Very high | Most dangerous |
| Dark / semi-sweet chocolate | High | Dangerous |
| Milk chocolate | Lower | Risky in larger amounts |
| White chocolate | Negligible | Fat/sugar risk, not theobromine |

Toxicity depends on **how much** was eaten and **your dog's weight** — a square of dark chocolate is a very different situation for a Chihuahua than a Labrador.

## What to do right now

1. **Don't panic, but act fast.** The best outcomes come from treating *before* symptoms start.
2. **Work out the details**: the type of chocolate, roughly how much, and your dog's weight. Keep the wrapper.
3. **Call your vet or a pet poison helpline immediately.** They'll assess the risk from the numbers.
4. **Follow their guidance.** If it was very recent, they may want to see your dog to **induce vomiting** and give **activated charcoal** — do not try to make your dog vomit at home unless a professional tells you to and how.

> The window to act is early. Calling before signs appear can be the difference between a simple outpatient visit and intensive hospital care.

## Warning signs of chocolate poisoning

Signs typically start within **6–12 hours** and can last a couple of days:

- Vomiting and diarrhoea
- **Restlessness, hyperactivity, pacing**
- Increased thirst and urination
- **Fast or irregular heart rate**
- High body temperature, panting
- **Muscle tremors**
- In severe cases, **seizures** and collapse

## Treatment

There's no specific antidote — treatment is about removing what hasn't been absorbed and supporting the body:

- **Decontamination** — inducing vomiting and giving activated charcoal (sometimes repeated)
- **IV fluids** to support the heart and help excretion
- **Medications** to control heart rate, tremors and seizures
- **Monitoring** until theobromine clears

Most dogs recover well **when treated promptly**. Prevention is easy: keep all chocolate — especially baking supplies and cocoa — well out of reach, and warn guests not to share treats.
