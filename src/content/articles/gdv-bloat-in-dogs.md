Bloat — properly called **gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV)** — is one of the true "minutes matter" emergencies in dogs. The stomach fills with gas and then rotates on itself, trapping the gas, cutting off its own blood supply, and pressing on major blood vessels. Without emergency treatment, a dog can go from normal to critical in under two hours.

## What actually happens during GDV

There are two parts to the name:

- **Dilatation** — the stomach balloons with gas and fluid.
- **Volvulus** — the bloated stomach twists (rotates), sealing off both the entrance and exit.

Once twisted, the stomach's blood supply is strangled and the tissue begins to die. The swollen stomach also compresses the large vein returning blood to the heart, sending the dog into shock. This is why GDV is fatal so fast.

## The warning signs — know these by heart

Call your vet immediately if you see:

- A **swollen, hard, or distended belly** (often behind the ribs)
- **Unproductive retching** — trying to vomit but nothing (or only foam) comes up
- **Excessive drooling**
- **Restlessness and pacing** — unable to get comfortable
- **Hunched posture** or looking at their abdomen
- **Rapid, shallow breathing**
- **Pale gums, weakness, or collapse** (late, dangerous signs)

> Unproductive retching plus a bloated belly in a large-breed dog is GDV until proven otherwise. Do not wait.

## Which dogs are most at risk

GDV is most common in **large, deep-chested breeds**:

| Higher risk | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Giant / deep-chested | Great Dane, Weimaraner, St. Bernard, Irish Setter |
| Large working breeds | German Shepherd, Standard Poodle, Boxer, Doberman |

Other risk factors: being older, eating one large meal daily, eating very fast, using raised feeding bowls, stress, and having a first-degree relative that had GDV.

## What the vet will do

Treatment is urgent and follows a clear sequence:

1. **Stabilise for shock** — IV fluids and pain relief.
2. **Decompress the stomach** — releasing trapped gas via a tube or needle.
3. **Surgery** — untwisting the stomach, checking for dead tissue, and performing a **gastropexy** (tacking the stomach to the body wall to stop it twisting again).

## Can bloat be prevented?

You can lower the risk:

- Feed **two or three smaller meals** a day instead of one large one.
- Slow down fast eaters with a **slow-feeder bowl**.
- Avoid heavy exercise right before and after meals.
- For high-risk breeds, ask your vet about a **preventive gastropexy** — often done at the same time as spay/neuter.

Prevention lowers the odds, but no measure is perfect. The most important thing you can do is **recognise the signs early and act fast.**
