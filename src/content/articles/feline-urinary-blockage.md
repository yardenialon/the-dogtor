Of all the emergencies a cat owner should recognise, a **urinary blockage** is one of the most urgent — and one of the most commonly missed, because the early signs look like ordinary "toilet trouble." A **blocked cat is a genuine, hours-count emergency.**

## What "blocked" means

A urinary obstruction is when something — usually a plug of crystals, mucus and inflammatory cells, or a bladder stone — physically blocks the urethra so urine **can't leave the body**. It's most common in **male cats** because their urethra is long and narrow.

When urine can't get out:

- The bladder swells, becoming hard and intensely painful.
- Waste toxins that should be excreted build up in the blood.
- **Potassium rises**, which can cause a dangerous, potentially fatal disturbance of the heart rhythm.

This is why a complete blockage can kill within **24–48 hours**.

## 🚩 Warning signs — act immediately

- **Frequent trips to the litter box** producing **little or no urine**
- **Straining and crying** while trying to urinate
- **Licking at the genitals** repeatedly
- **Restlessness, pacing, or hiding** (a sign of pain)
- **Vomiting**, drooling, going off food
- A **firm, painful belly**
- In late stages: **weakness, collapse, a slow heart rate**

> Owners sometimes mistake straining for constipation. If you're not sure — assume it could be a blockage and get to a vet. It's the safer call.

## Why you can't wait

There is **no safe way to relieve a blockage at home.** The build-up of potassium and toxins is invisible from the outside and can stop the heart. A cat that seems "just uncomfortable" in the evening can be critical by morning. If you suspect a blockage, phone your vet or the nearest emergency clinic **now** and head in.

## What the vet does

1. **Stabilise** — bloodwork to check potassium and kidney values, IV fluids, and treating any dangerous heart-rhythm changes first.
2. **Relieve the obstruction** — passing a urinary catheter under sedation or anaesthesia to flush out the plug and empty the bladder.
3. **Hospitalise** — the catheter usually stays in for a day or two with IV fluids while the bladder recovers and values normalise.
4. **Recovery plan** — pain relief, and sometimes medication to relax the urethra.

## Preventing a recurrence

Re-blocking is a real risk, especially early on. To lower it:

- **Increase water intake** — wet food, water fountains, multiple water stations
- **A vet-recommended urinary diet** to reduce crystal formation
- **Reduce stress** — stress is a major driver of feline lower urinary tract disease. Provide enough clean litter boxes (one per cat, plus one), calm routines, and enrichment.
- **Follow-up checks** as advised

Knowing these signs — and taking them seriously — is genuinely life-saving for cats.
