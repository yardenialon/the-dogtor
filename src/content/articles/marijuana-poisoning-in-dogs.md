As cannabis becomes more common in homes, so do accidental poisonings in dogs — from dried buds, oils, and especially **edibles** left within reach. Dogs are far more sensitive to THC than people, so even a small amount can cause obvious signs. If your dog ate weed, the key message is: **don't wait, and don't panic — call a vet and be honest about what happened.**

## Why THC affects dogs so strongly

Dogs have a high number of cannabinoid receptors in their brains, which makes them **much more sensitive to THC** than humans. What's a mild effect in a person can leave a dog heavily sedated and unsteady. They also can't tell you what's wrong, so recognising the signs matters.

## Signs your dog has eaten cannabis

Signs usually appear within **30–90 minutes** and can last a day or more:

- **Wobbliness and sedation** — the classic drunk, unsteady walk
- **Dribbling urine** — a very typical and telling sign
- **Startle reactions** — over-sensitive to sound or touch
- **Dilated pupils, glazed eyes**
- **Drooling, vomiting**
- **Slow heart rate and low body temperature**
- In severe cases: agitation, tremors, or collapse

## The hidden danger: what's *in* the edible

This is the part owners underestimate. A THC edible may **also** contain two things that are much more dangerous to dogs than the cannabis:

| Ingredient | Risk to dogs |
| --- | --- |
| **Chocolate** | Theobromine poisoning — heart and nervous system |
| **Xylitol** (sweetener) | Rapid, life-threatening blood-sugar crash and liver damage |

That's why you should always **keep the packaging** and tell your vet the exact product.

## What to do right now

1. **Remove** any remaining product so your dog can't eat more.
2. **Call your vet or a pet poison line immediately.** Tell them the form, amount and timing.
3. **Be honest.** Vets are focused on helping your pet — accurate information leads to the right treatment.
4. **Don't induce vomiting** unless a professional tells you to; in a wobbly, sedated dog it can be dangerous.
5. **Keep your dog warm, quiet and safe** from stairs or furniture they could fall from.

> Most dogs recover fully with prompt supportive care. The danger comes from waiting, from a large dose, or from toxic edible ingredients like chocolate and xylitol.

## When it's an emergency

Get emergency care straight away if your dog:

- **Can't stand, is unresponsive, or is losing consciousness**
- Is having **tremors or seizures**
- Ate an **edible with chocolate or xylitol**
- Is a **small dog or puppy** that may have eaten a large amount
