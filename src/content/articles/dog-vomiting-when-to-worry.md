Almost every dog vomits at some point, and most of the time it isn't serious. The hard part for owners is knowing when a bit of sick is nothing to worry about — and when it's a signal to pick up the phone. This guide gives you clear rules.

## First: vomiting vs. regurgitation

These look similar but mean different things to your vet:

- **Vomiting** is active. You'll see heaving, drooling and abdominal effort. What comes up is usually partly digested and yellow-tinged (bile).
- **Regurgitation** is passive. Undigested food or water simply comes back up, often right after eating, with no heaving.

Telling your vet which one you saw genuinely helps narrow down the cause.

## When vomiting is usually NOT an emergency

You can often monitor at home if your dog:

- Vomited **once or twice** but is otherwise **bright, alert and playful**
- Is **drinking and keeping water down**
- Has **normal gums** (pink and moist) and normal energy
- Has no diarrhoea, no bloating, and isn't painful

**What to do:** withhold food for 6–12 hours (not water), then offer a small **bland meal** — for example plain boiled chicken and white rice. Feed small amounts often for a day, then transition back to normal food.

## 🚩 When to call the vet now

Contact your veterinarian promptly if you notice any of these:

- **Repeated vomiting** (several times in a few hours) or vomiting that won't stop
- **Can't keep water down**
- **Blood** in the vomit (fresh red, or dark "coffee-ground" material)
- **A swollen, hard belly** — especially with unproductive retching (possible [bloat/GDV](/articles/gdv-bloat-in-dogs), a true emergency)
- **Lethargy, weakness or collapse**
- **Signs of pain**, a hunched posture, or crying
- **Suspected poisoning** or that your dog **swallowed an object** (toy, sock, bone)
- **Diarrhoea at the same time**, especially with blood
- Vomiting in a **young puppy, senior dog, or a dog with other illness** — these dehydrate and crash faster

## Common causes of vomiting in dogs

- **Dietary indiscretion** — eating rubbish, table scraps, or too much too fast
- **Sudden diet change**
- **Intestinal parasites**
- **Infections** (including parvovirus in unvaccinated puppies)
- **Swallowed foreign objects** causing a blockage
- **Toxins** — chocolate, xylitol, grapes/raisins, plants, medications
- **Organ disease** — kidney, liver, pancreatitis
- **Metabolic/hormonal disease** — such as diabetes or Addison's

## How the vet works it out

Depending on the picture, your vet may recommend a physical exam, blood tests, and imaging (X-ray or ultrasound) to rule out a blockage or organ disease. Treatment ranges from anti-nausea medication and fluids to surgery if there's an obstruction.

The bottom line: **an occasional vomit in a happy dog is usually fine, but repeated vomiting or vomiting plus any other warning sign deserves a call.**
