Watching your dog suddenly collapse is terrifying — and in the moment it's hard to know whether you've just seen a **faint (syncope)** or a **seizure**. They can look similar, but telling them apart matters, because they point to **very different problems**: fainting usually comes from the **heart**, while a seizure comes from the **brain**. Here's how to tell fast.

## The fastest way to tell them apart

| | **Fainting (syncope)** | **Seizure** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Collapse** | Goes **limp / floppy** | Often **stiff**, then paddling |
| **Duration** | Seconds | Usually 1–3 minutes |
| **Movement** | Little or none | Paddling, chomping, twitching |
| **Drooling / chomping** | No | Common |
| **Bladder / bowel** | Usually controlled | Often loses control |
| **Recovery** | **Instant and normal** | **Confused, dazed** for minutes–hours |
| **Trigger** | Excitement, exertion, coughing | Often none obvious |
| **Points to** | Heart / circulation | Brain |

The single most useful clue is the **recovery**: a fainting dog pops back up and acts normal almost at once, while a dog coming out of a seizure is usually **disoriented, wobbly or dazed** for a while.

## What fainting (syncope) looks like

Syncope happens when the brain is briefly starved of blood or oxygen — usually a **heart or circulation** issue. The dog:

- Collapses **limp**, often after excitement, exercise or a coughing fit
- Is unconscious for only **a few seconds**
- **Gets straight back up** and seems fine

Because it can signal **heart rhythm problems or heart disease**, fainting always deserves a vet work-up.

## What a seizure looks like

A seizure is abnormal electrical activity in the **brain**. A typical (generalised) seizure involves:

- **Stiffening** then **paddling** of the legs
- **Jaw chomping**, drooling, vocalising
- **Loss of bladder or bowel control**
- A **confused, restless recovery** (the "post-ictal" phase)

## What to do in the moment

**If it looks like a seizure:**
1. Stay calm and **keep your hands away from the mouth** (dogs don't swallow their tongues, and you can be bitten).
2. **Clear the area** of hard or sharp objects.
3. **Time it** and reduce noise and light.
4. Let it run its course, then **call your vet**.

**If it looks like a faint:**
1. Keep your dog **calm and still**.
2. Note **what they were doing** just before (coughing? excited? exercising?).
3. **Call your vet** — fainting needs a heart check.

> Whatever it looks like, **film it if you safely can.** A short video of the episode and the recovery is often the difference between a guess and a diagnosis.

## When it's an emergency

Go to an emergency hospital **immediately** if:

- A **seizure lasts more than ~5 minutes**
- **Several seizures** happen close together without full recovery
- Your dog **doesn't fully recover**
- **Fainting keeps recurring**

Even a single, brief episode with full recovery should get a **prompt vet visit** to find the underlying cause.

## The bottom line

Fainting is floppy, brief and heart-related; a seizure is stiff-then-paddling, longer, and brain-related — and the recovery tells you the most. Both need veterinary attention. Watch the full explainer above, and see more [dog health guides](/species/dogs) or our [emergency guide](/emergencies).
