If your dog makes a sudden, dry cough that sounds like a **goose honk**, especially when they get excited or pull on the leash, one of the most likely explanations is **tracheal collapse**. It looks alarming, but with the right management most dogs live comfortably for years.

## What is tracheal collapse?

The trachea (windpipe) is held open by C-shaped rings of cartilage. In some dogs — mainly **small and toy breeds** — that cartilage weakens over time and the windpipe flattens, especially when air is rushing through it. As the walls touch, they irritate each other and set off that distinctive honking cough.

It is **chronic and progressive**, meaning it tends to develop slowly and continue over the dog's life. The goal of treatment is to control the cough and slow the progression, not to reverse the cartilage change.

## The tell-tale sign: a goose-honk cough

The cough is:

- **Dry and harsh** — not wet or productive
- **Honking** — often described as sounding like a goose
- **Triggered** by excitement, exercise, drinking, pressure on the neck (a collar), heat and humidity

Many owners first notice it after a walk, when the dog gets up excited, or on a hot day.

## Which dogs are at risk?

| Risk factor | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Small / toy breed | Yorkies, Pomeranians, Chihuahuas, Maltese, Toy Poodles are predisposed |
| Middle-aged to older | Cartilage weakens with age |
| Obesity | Extra weight and fat around the airway worsen breathing |
| Collar use | Direct pressure on the windpipe triggers coughing |
| Heart disease / airway irritants | Smoke, dust and allergens inflame the airway |

## How vets diagnose it

A vet will often suspect tracheal collapse from the breed, age and the sound of the cough. Confirmation may involve **X-rays** taken during both breathing in and out, **fluoroscopy** (a moving X-ray) to watch the trachea in real time, or airway examination. Your vet will also rule out other causes of coughing such as heart disease, kennel cough and bronchitis.

## How tracheal collapse is treated

Most dogs are managed **medically and with lifestyle changes**:

- **Weight loss** — often the single most effective step for an overweight dog
- **Harness, never a collar** — removes pressure from the windpipe
- **Cough suppressants and anti-inflammatories** — to break the cough–irritation cycle
- **Avoiding triggers** — heat, smoke, over-excitement and strenuous exercise
- **Managing any co-existing conditions** such as heart disease

For dogs that don't respond to medical management, a specialist may consider a **stent** placed inside the trachea or **surgical rings** placed around it.

> A collapsing trachea can't be cured, but with weight control, a harness and the right medication, the vast majority of dogs cough far less and stay comfortable.

## When the cough is an emergency

Most tracheal-collapse coughing is not an emergency — but call your vet or an emergency hospital **immediately** if your dog:

- Has **blue, grey or purple gums or tongue**
- Is **gasping** or struggling to breathe
- **Collapses** or faints
- Has a coughing fit that **won't stop**

These signs mean the airway is severely narrowed and your dog needs urgent oxygen and stabilisation.
