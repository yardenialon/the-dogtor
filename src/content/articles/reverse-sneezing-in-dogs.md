Few things startle a dog owner like the first reverse sneeze: your dog suddenly freezes, extends its neck, and makes a series of loud, honking, snorting sounds as if it can't breathe. The good news is that **reverse sneezing is usually harmless** — and there's a simple trick to help stop an episode.

## What reverse sneezing actually is

A normal sneeze pushes air **out**. A reverse sneeze pulls air rapidly **in** through the nose, in a burst of snorting sounds. It's a reflex triggered by **irritation of the soft palate and upper throat**. During an episode a dog typically:

- Stands still and **extends the head and neck**
- Makes a **honking, snorting or gasping** sound
- Looks briefly alarmed — then returns to completely normal

Episodes usually last a **few seconds to about a minute**.

## The simple home remedy

To help end an episode gently, the goal is to make your dog **swallow**, which relaxes the throat:

- **Briefly cover the nostrils** for a second so your dog swallows, or
- **Lightly massage the throat** in a downward motion, and
- **Keep calm and reassuring** — stress can prolong it

> Be gentle. You're prompting a swallow, not restricting breathing. If in doubt, simply keeping your dog calm and still is enough — most episodes stop by themselves.

## What triggers it

Common triggers include:

- **Excitement** or sudden activity
- **Pulling against a collar** (a harness can help)
- **Eating or drinking too fast**
- **Irritants** — dust, pollen, sprays, strong smells
- **Post-nasal drip** from mild allergies

**Small breeds** and **flat-faced (brachycephalic) breeds** are especially prone.

## Reverse sneezing vs. a collapsing trachea

Because both make odd honking sounds, owners often mix them up:

| Reverse sneezing | Tracheal collapse |
| --- | --- |
| Rapid **inward** snorting fit | Dry **outward** honking cough |
| Starts and stops suddenly | Recurring cough, often with exertion |
| Usually harmless | Chronic, progressive; needs management |

If you're not sure which your dog has, our guide to the [goose-honk cough of tracheal collapse](/articles/tracheal-collapse-in-dogs) can help — and a vet can confirm.

## When to see a vet

Most reverse sneezing needs no treatment. Book a vet visit if episodes are:

- **Frequent or getting worse**
- **Prolonged**, or your dog struggles to breathe **between** episodes
- Accompanied by **nasal discharge, bleeding, or pawing at the face**

These can signal allergies, nasal mites, a foreign body, or other conditions worth investigating.

Reverse sneezing sounds scary but is usually a harmless reflex that passes in under a minute — and a gentle swallow-prompting trick often stops it. Watch the full explainer above for the technique, and explore more [dog health guides](/species/dogs).
