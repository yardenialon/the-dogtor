**IMHA** stands for **immune-mediated haemolytic anaemia**. In plain terms: the dog's own immune system mistakenly identifies its red blood cells as the enemy and destroys them. Because red blood cells carry oxygen, the dog becomes severely anaemic — and it can happen dangerously fast.

## Why IMHA is so serious

Red blood cells deliver oxygen to every tissue. When they're destroyed faster than the bone marrow can replace them, organs start to starve of oxygen. On top of that, IMHA makes the blood prone to forming **clots (thromboembolism)**, which is one of the most dangerous complications. This combination is why IMHA is treated as an emergency.

## Warning signs to watch for

- **Pale gums** (check the colour — should be pink, not white)
- **Yellow tinge** to the gums, eyes or skin (jaundice) as red cells break down
- **Weakness, lethargy, exercise intolerance** — tiring very easily
- **Fast breathing and a racing heart**
- **Dark orange, brown or red urine**
- **Reduced appetite**, sometimes fever or collapse

> Pale or yellow gums with sudden weakness is a red-flag combination. Get to a vet the same day.

## Primary vs. secondary IMHA

- **Primary (idiopathic)** — no underlying trigger is found. This is the most common form in dogs.
- **Secondary** — triggered by something else: infections (including tick-borne disease), certain drugs, cancer, or occasionally a recent vaccine. Finding and removing the trigger is part of treatment.

## How it's diagnosed

Vets confirm IMHA with blood tests that show:

- **Anaemia** (a low red blood cell count / packed cell volume)
- Signs the body is destroying red cells — such as **autoagglutination** (red cells clumping), **spherocytes** on a blood smear, and a positive **saline agglutination test**
- Evidence the bone marrow is trying to respond (a *regenerative* anaemia)

Further tests look for an underlying cause and check for complications like clots.

## Treatment

IMHA treatment is intensive and usually needs hospitalisation:

1. **Immunosuppression** — corticosteroids such as prednisolone are the backbone, often combined with a second immune-suppressing drug in more severe cases.
2. **Blood transfusion** — if the anaemia is life-threatening.
3. **Anti-clot medication** — to reduce the high risk of thromboembolism.
4. **Supportive care** — IV fluids, nutrition and close monitoring.

Recovery can take weeks to months, and medication is tapered slowly to avoid relapse. Because relapses can happen, dogs need regular blood-test rechecks. With early, aggressive treatment many dogs do recover — which is exactly why recognising the signs early matters so much.
