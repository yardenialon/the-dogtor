An occasional bit of drool from a blissed-out, purring cat is usually nothing to worry about. But **sudden, heavy or constant drooling** — the medical term is **hypersalivation** or ptyalism — is one of those signs cats give that's easy to overlook and often points to a real problem.

## When drooling is (probably) normal

Some cats drool a little when they are:

- **Deeply relaxed** — purring, kneading and completely content
- **Anticipating food** they really want
- **Briefly** reacting to a new smell or taste

The key word is **small and short-lived**, with a happy, comfortable cat.

## When drooling is a warning sign

Be concerned if the drooling is **sudden, heavy, ongoing, or smelly**, or comes with any of these:

- **Not eating** or dropping food
- **Pawing at the mouth** or rubbing the face
- **Bad breath** or blood-tinged saliva
- **Lethargy**, hiding, or a hunched, uncomfortable posture

## The common causes

| Cause | What's happening |
| --- | --- |
| **Dental & mouth disease** | Painful teeth, resorptive lesions, inflamed or ulcerated gums — a leading cause |
| **Nausea** | Kidney disease, tummy upset and other illnesses make cats salivate |
| **Toxins & bitter tastes** | Licking a toxic plant or chemical, or a bad-tasting medication |
| **Foreign object** | Something (like string or a bone fragment) stuck in the mouth or throat |
| **Pain or injury** | An oral injury, burn or wound |
| **Heat stress / neurological** | Less common, but possible |

Because so many of these cause **mouth pain**, drooling and **not eating** frequently go together — and that combination should never be ignored, since a cat that stops eating can become seriously ill quickly.

## When to get help urgently

Treat it as an **emergency** and call a vet or emergency hospital right away if drooling comes with:

- **Difficulty breathing** or collapse
- A **suspected poisoning** (chewed plant, chemical, human medication)
- A **visible foreign object**, or frantic pawing and inability to swallow

If you think your cat swallowed something toxic, don't wait for more symptoms — contact your vet or a pet poison line immediately.

## What the vet will do

Your vet will start with a careful **mouth and physical exam** — often the cause is found right there in a painful tooth or inflamed gum. Depending on findings, they may recommend a **dental assessment under anaesthesia**, bloodwork to check for nausea-causing illness like kidney disease, or imaging to look for a foreign object.

> The bottom line: brief drooling from a happy, relaxed cat is usually fine. New, heavy, smelly drooling — especially with a reduced appetite — is a sign to get your cat checked, because mouth pain and nausea are common and very treatable once found.
