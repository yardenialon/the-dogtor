For rabbits, **not eating is never "just fussiness."** A rabbit that goes off its food is showing one of the most important warning signs in exotic pet medicine: possible **gastrointestinal (GI) stasis**, sometimes called the "silent killer."

## What GI stasis is

GI stasis is when the normal, constant movement of a rabbit's digestive tract **slows down or stops**. Rabbits are built to graze all day, and their gut relies on a steady flow of fibre and a balanced population of gut bacteria.

When the gut slows:

- Food and gas **build up** and the contents dry out.
- The **balance of gut bacteria is disrupted**, and harmful gas-producing bacteria can take over.
- This causes **painful bloating** — which makes the rabbit eat even less, feeding a vicious cycle.

Crucially, **rabbits cannot vomit**, so anything that goes wrong in the gut has only one way through. That's why stasis becomes life-threatening so quickly.

## 🚩 Warning signs — this is urgent

Contact an exotics/rabbit-savvy vet urgently if your rabbit shows:

- **Not eating**, or a sudden drop in appetite
- **No droppings, or much smaller/fewer** droppings than normal
- **Hunched posture**, pressing the belly to the floor, or reluctance to move
- **Lethargy** and hiding
- **Teeth grinding** (a sign of pain in rabbits)
- A **bloated or gurgling** belly

> A rabbit that hasn't eaten or passed droppings in about 12 hours is an emergency. Do not wait to see if it improves overnight.

## Why it's an emergency, not a "wait and see"

Because the gut ecosystem is so delicate and rabbits can't vomit, a rabbit can deteriorate from "a bit quiet" to critical within a day. There's also an important distinction only a vet can make: **stasis (a slowed gut)** versus a **true blockage (an obstruction)**, which can be surgical. Treating them wrongly is dangerous — which is why home treatment isn't safe.

## What's usually behind it

GI stasis is almost always a symptom of another problem:

- **Dental disease** — overgrown or spurred teeth cause pain that stops eating (very common)
- **Pain** from any source
- **Stress** — a change in environment, a new pet, travel, loud noise
- **Low-fibre diet** — too many pellets/treats, not enough hay
- **Dehydration**
- **Another underlying illness**

## Treatment

Veterinary treatment typically includes:

- **Pain relief** — pain is a major driver, so this is central
- **Fluids** — to rehydrate the gut contents
- **Gut-motility medication** (once a blockage is ruled out)
- **Assisted (syringe) feeding** with a fibre-rich recovery formula — rabbits must keep getting nutrition
- **Finding and treating the underlying cause**, such as a dental problem

## Prevention

The single best protection is diet:

- **Unlimited grass hay** — the foundation of a rabbit's diet; it keeps the gut moving *and* wears down teeth
- **Fresh leafy greens** daily
- **Limit pellets and sugary treats**
- **Constant fresh water** and plenty of exercise
- **Reduce stress** and get **regular dental checks**

Learn to watch your rabbit's appetite and droppings every day — for rabbits, those two things are a live readout of their health.
