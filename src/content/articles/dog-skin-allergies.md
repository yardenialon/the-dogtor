An itchy dog is a miserable dog — and a common one. If your dog is constantly scratching, licking its paws, chewing at its skin, or getting one skin or ear infection after another, allergies are near the top of the list. The good news: while allergies usually can't be *cured*, they can almost always be *controlled*.

## What allergy signs look like

- **Itching**: scratching, licking, chewing, rubbing on furniture
- **Favourite itchy spots**: paws, belly, armpits, groin, face and ears
- **Red, inflamed skin**; hair loss from over-grooming
- **Recurring ear infections** (see [dog ear infections](/articles/dog-ear-infections))
- **Repeated skin infections** — bacterial or yeast
- **Saliva staining** — reddish-brown fur on paws from constant licking

Timing is a clue: **seasonal** itch often points to environmental allergies, while **year-round** itch can suggest food or indoor allergens.

## The three main types

### 1. Flea allergy dermatitis
The most common allergy in dogs. Allergic dogs react to proteins in flea *saliva*, so **a single bite** can set off days of intense itching — usually around the tail base and back end. You may never see a flea. **Year-round flea control is the foundation** of managing any itchy dog.

### 2. Environmental allergy (atopic dermatitis)
An allergy to things in the environment — pollens, dust mites, mould spores, grasses. It often starts between 1–3 years of age and may be seasonal at first, becoming year-round over time.

### 3. Food allergy
A reaction to a protein in the diet (commonly beef, chicken, dairy). Food allergy can cause itching **and** sometimes digestive signs, and it isn't seasonal.

## How allergies are diagnosed

There's no single quick test. Vets work through it methodically:

1. **Rule out parasites** with strict flea/mite control.
2. **Treat any infections** that have flared up on the itchy skin.
3. **Elimination diet trial** (8–12 weeks) if food allergy is suspected — a novel or hydrolysed protein, and *nothing else*.
4. **Diagnose atopy** based on history and by ruling out the rest; allergy testing (blood or intradermal) is used to *formulate immunotherapy*, not to make the diagnosis.

## Treatments that actually help

Management usually combines several tools:

- **Strict, year-round parasite control** — non-negotiable
- **Anti-itch medication** — modern options (such as targeted anti-itch drugs) work well and safely for many dogs
- **Treating flare-up infections** promptly
- **Skin barrier support** — omega-3 fatty acids, medicated shampoos, regular bathing
- **Immunotherapy ("allergy vaccines")** for environmental allergy — retraining the immune system over time
- **Diet change** for confirmed food allergy

The aim isn't perfection — it's keeping your dog comfortable, with fewer and milder flare-ups. Partnering with your vet on a long-term plan is what makes the biggest difference.
