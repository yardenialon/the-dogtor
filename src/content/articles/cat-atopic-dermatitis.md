If your cat is **constantly scratching, licking themselves bald, or developing scabs**, it's easy to assume it's just grooming or stress. But one of the most common causes of an itchy cat is **feline atopic skin syndrome** (atopic dermatitis) — an allergic skin disease that is very manageable once it's diagnosed.

## What is feline atopic dermatitis?

It's an **allergic reaction to environmental allergens** — pollen, dust mites, mould spores, grasses. In a sensitive cat, the immune system overreacts to these harmless substances, the skin barrier becomes inflamed, and the result is relentless itch.

Because cats are private groomers, owners often **don't see the scratching** — they only notice the results: thinning fur, bald patches, or scabs.

## The four faces of the itchy cat

Feline allergic skin disease shows up in several classic patterns, and a cat may have more than one:

- **Self-induced hair loss** — symmetrical thinning, often on the belly and inner legs, from overgrooming
- **Miliary dermatitis** — lots of tiny crusty bumps you feel like grit along the back
- **Head and neck itching** — intense scratching, scabs and sores around the face and ears
- **Eosinophilic lesions** — raised red sores or swollen lips and thighs

## What else can look like this?

Before blaming the environment, vets rule out the two great mimics:

| Cause | Key clue |
| --- | --- |
| **Fleas** | Even one bite can trigger intense itch in an allergic cat; often over the tail base |
| **Food allergy** | Year-round itching, sometimes with tummy upset; needs a diet trial |
| **Environmental atopy** | May be seasonal at first, then year-round; a diagnosis of exclusion |

This is why **strict flea control** and sometimes a **food elimination trial** come first — they're the only way to separate these overlapping causes.

## How vets diagnose it

There is **no single blood test** that says "atopic dermatitis." Diagnosis is a step-by-step process:

1. Rule out fleas and other parasites with strict prevention
2. Treat any secondary bacterial or yeast skin infection
3. Run a food elimination trial if itching is year-round
4. Once other causes are excluded, consider **allergy testing** to identify specific environmental triggers

## How itchy cats are treated

The goal is to **control the itch and repair the skin**, not to find a one-time cure:

- **Year-round flea prevention** — non-negotiable for allergic cats
- **Anti-itch medication** prescribed by your vet to calm the immune response
- **Treating secondary infections** that develop in damaged skin
- **Allergen-specific immunotherapy** (allergy shots or drops) in some cats, to reduce sensitivity over time
- **Omega-3 supplements and skin support** as adjuncts

> Atopic dermatitis is lifelong, but with the right plan most cats become comfortable, stop overgrooming, and regrow their coat.

## When to see a vet

Book an appointment if your cat is licking or scratching enough to **lose fur, break the skin, or develop scabs and sores**. Early treatment prevents secondary infections and a lot of discomfort.
