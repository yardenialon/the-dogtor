If your dog is shaking its head, scratching at an ear, or has that unmistakable yeasty smell, there's a good chance it has an ear infection — known medically as **otitis externa** (inflammation of the outer ear canal). It's one of the most common reasons dogs visit the vet, and one of the most misunderstood.

## Signs of an ear infection

- **Head shaking** and tilting
- **Scratching or rubbing** the ear (on furniture, the floor, with a paw)
- **Redness and swelling** inside the ear flap and canal
- **A bad smell**
- **Discharge** — brown, yellow or waxy
- **Pain** when you touch the ear, or the dog pulling away
- In severe or inner-ear cases: a persistent **head tilt**, loss of balance, or walking in circles

## What actually causes it

Here's the part many owners miss: an ear infection is often the *visible symptom* of a deeper problem. The ear canal is warm and moist, so once the skin inside becomes inflamed, yeast (*Malassezia*) and bacteria overgrow.

Common underlying and contributing causes:

- **Allergies** — the number-one reason for *recurring* infections. Food and environmental allergies inflame the ear lining.
- **Ear anatomy** — floppy-eared and hairy-eared breeds (Cocker Spaniels, Poodles, Retrievers) trap moisture.
- **Moisture** — swimming or bathing without drying the ears ("swimmer's ear").
- **Ear mites** — more common in puppies; produce a dark, crumbly discharge.
- **Foreign bodies** — grass seeds and awns, especially in summer.
- **Growths or polyps** in the canal.

> If your dog gets ear infection after ear infection, the real question isn't "which drops?" — it's "what's driving them?" That's usually an allergy.

## Why you should see a vet first

It's tempting to reach for a cleaner or leftover medication, but this can backfire. If the **eardrum is ruptured** (which you can't see from the outside), certain products can damage hearing and balance. A vet will:

- Examine the canal with an **otoscope** and check the eardrum
- Take a **swab and look at it under the microscope** (cytology) to see whether it's yeast, bacteria, or both — this decides the treatment
- Choose the right medication and show you how to clean correctly

## Treatment

- **Ear cleaning** to remove debris so medication can work
- **Topical medication** — usually a combination product targeting yeast, bacteria and inflammation
- **Oral medication** in severe or deep infections
- **Treating the underlying cause** — the crucial step for stopping recurrence, often meaning an [allergy work-up](/articles/dog-skin-allergies)
- **A recheck** to confirm the infection has fully cleared

Get the infection *and* its driver under control, and you break the frustrating cycle of ears that never quite stay better.
